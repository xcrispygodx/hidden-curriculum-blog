Strategy of Tension:
NATO's Secret Stay-Behind Armies

25 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

In the early 1950s, as the Cold War solidified into a permanent confrontation between East and West, the intelligence agencies of the United States and the United Kingdom began building a secret army across Western Europe. The program was known by various codenames in different countries — Gladio in Italy, SDRA8 in Belgium, Absalon in Denmark, Stay-Behind in the Netherlands — but its purpose was uniform: to create clandestine networks of armed operatives who, in the event of a Soviet invasion, would wage guerrilla warfare behind enemy lines. The concept was simple. The implementation was anything but.

The stay-behind networks were funded by the CIA and MI6, organized through NATO's Allied Clandestine Committee and the Clandestine Planning Committee, and operated across at least 16 Western European nations. The recruits were drawn from former military officers, intelligence operatives, and — in a pattern that would recur with disturbing regularity — former Nazis who had been repositioned as anti-communist assets. Hans Rues, a former SS officer, was recruited into the German stay-behind network. Walter Kopp, another former Nazi, served as a liaison between the German intelligence service and the NATO clandestine committees. The use of former Nazis in the stay-behind networks was not an oversight. It was a deliberate strategy — the same strategy that drove Operation Paperclip: the repurposing of Nazi expertise for Cold War objectives.

But the stay-behind armies never fought the Soviet invasion they were designed to resist. Instead, they fought a different war — a war against the citizens of the countries in which they operated, a war of terror and manipulation designed to prevent left-wing political parties from gaining power through democratic elections. The strategy was called the "strategy of tension" — the deliberate creation of fear and instability in order to shift the political center of gravity to the right. And the weapons of this strategy were not guerrilla tactics or sabotage. They were bombings. Massacres. Assassinations. The murder of ordinary civilians in train stations, on trains, in public squares — carried out by the secret armies and blamed on left-wing organizations that had nothing to do with the attacks.

◆ Italy: The Laboratory of Terror

Italy was the primary theater of Gladio operations and the country where the strategy of tension produced its most devastating results. The Italian stay-behind network, known as Gladio, was established in 1956 under the auspices of the Italian military intelligence service SIFAR (later SID, then SISMI) with CIA funding and operational support. The network recruited between 600 and 1,000 operatives, most of them former soldiers and police officers, and equipped them with weapons caches hidden in underground bunkers across the Italian countryside. The weapons included rifles, explosives, communications equipment, and — in a detail that would later prove significant — detonators manufactured by NATO ordnance depots.

The first major terrorist attack attributed to the strategy of tension was the Piazza Fontana bombing on December 12, 1969. A bomb exploded inside the Banca Nazionale dell'Agricoltura in Milan's Piazza Fontana, killing 17 people and wounding 88. The attack was initially blamed on anarchists — one of whom, Giuseppe Pinelli, died after falling from a fourth-floor window of the Milan police headquarters under circumstances that remain disputed. Another anarchist, Pietro Valpreda, was arrested and held for three years before being acquitted. The actual perpetrators were later identified as members of a neo-fascist group with connections to the Italian stay-behind network.

The pattern repeated. On May 31, 1972, a bomb killed three Carabinieri officers and a civilian in Peteano, near the Slovenian border. The attack was blamed on the Red Brigades, a left-wing terrorist organization. For years, the investigation went nowhere — deliberately. It was not until 1984 that the neo-fascist terrorist Vincenzo Vinciguerra was convicted of the Peteano bombing, and not until the early 1990s that Judge Felice Casson's investigation uncovered the role of the Italian military intelligence service in covering up the true perpetrators. Casson discovered that the explosives used at Peteano had come from a Gladio weapons cache — a cache that the intelligence service had known about and deliberately concealed from investigators.

◆ The Bologna Massacre: August 2, 1980

The deadliest terrorist attack in Italian history — and the single bloodiest act of political violence in Western Europe during the Cold War — occurred on August 2, 1980, at the Bologna Centrale railway station. At 10:25 AM, a suitcase bomb placed in the waiting room of the second-class section of the station detonated, killing 85 people and wounding more than 200. The explosion ripped through the station, collapsing the roof of the waiting room and sending shrapnel and debris across the platform. The dead included children, elderly passengers, and a young couple who had been sitting on their suitcases waiting for a train. The youngest victim was three years old.

The investigation into the Bologna massacre revealed connections to the Nuclei Armati Rivoluzionari (NAR), a neo-fascist terrorist organization with ties to the Italian stay-behind network and to the Propaganda Due (P2) Masonic Lodge. P2, led by Licio Gelli — a former fascist and intelligence asset — was a secret society that had enrolled nearly 1,000 members from Italy's political, military, financial, and media elite, including future Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi, the heads of all three Italian intelligence services, 195 officers of the Carabinieri, and 110 officers of the Army. P2 functioned as a shadow government, a parallel state that operated above and beyond the democratic institutions it had infiltrated.

The P2 connection is essential to understanding how the strategy of tension functioned. The bombings were not random acts of violence. They were coordinated operations that involved neo-fascist operatives (who built and placed the bombs), military intelligence officers (who provided weapons and logistical support from Gladio caches), Masonic Lodge members (who controlled the political and media response), and CIA handlers (who provided funding, strategic direction, and diplomatic cover). The system was vertically integrated — from the bomb maker to the Prime Minister's office, every element of the operation was controlled by the same network.

The Italicus Express bombing on August 4, 1974 — which killed 12 people and wounded 48 on a train traveling from Rome to Munich — followed the same pattern. The attack was initially claimed by a neo-fascist group but later attributed to unknown perpetrators. The investigation was obstructed at every level. Witnesses disappeared. Evidence was destroyed. Suspects were acquitted on technicalities. The Italicus bombing, like Piazza Fontana and Peteano before it, and like Bologna after it, remained technically unsolved for decades — not because the perpetrators were unknown, but because the institutional apparatus that protected them was too powerful to challenge.

◆ The Testimony of Vincenzo Vinciguerra

The most important testimony about the strategy of tension came not from investigators or journalists but from one of its perpetrators. Vincenzo Vinciguerra, a neo-fascist militant convicted of the 1972 Peteano bombing, gave a series of interviews from prison in the early 1990s that provided an insider's account of how the strategy operated. Vinciguerra's testimony was extraordinary not because it revealed previously unknown facts — much of what he said was already suspected — but because it articulated the logic of the strategy with a clarity that no official investigation had achieved.

"The plan was to create a state of emergency," Vinciguerra told Judge Casson. "The state had to have an external enemy against which to defend itself. The extreme right was used to create this emergency situation, to force the state to adopt more authoritarian measures. You had to attack civilians, the people, because the state had to be forced to take measures against the left." He described the relationship between the neo-fascist groups and the intelligence services as one of mutual dependency: the terrorists provided the violence, the intelligence services provided the cover, and both served the interests of the political establishment in preventing a shift to the left.

Vinciguerra was explicit about the role of the state in orchestrating terrorism. "When you throw a bomb and kill people, you expect to be arrested," he said. "But the people who threw the bombs with us were protected by the state. The investigation was obstructed by the state. The state was the organizer of the terror." He described how the neo-fascist groups received weapons, explosives, and operational instructions from military intelligence officers who identified themselves as members of the Gladio network. He named names. He provided dates and locations. And he was largely ignored — because his testimony implicated not just the terrorists but the institutions that had used them.

◆ Belgium: The Brabant Massacres

The strategy of tension was not confined to Italy. In Belgium, between 1982 and 1985, a series of exceptionally violent attacks — known collectively as the Brabant Massacres — killed 28 people and wounded 40 more. The attacks were carried out by a group that became known as the Nijvel Gang (or the Band of Nijvel), who targeted supermarkets, restaurants, and a weapons shop with military-grade weapons and extraordinary brutality.

The modus operandi of the Brabant Massacres bore the unmistakable signature of military training. The attackers used assault rifles with controlled bursts, maintained disciplined fire patterns, and employed tactical formations that indicated extensive training. They killed without apparent motive — in one attack at the Delhaize supermarket in Aalst on November 9, 1985, they shot 8 people dead and wounded 3 more, took nothing of value, and left in under 3 minutes. In another attack at the Delhaize supermarket in Overijse on September 27, 1985, they killed 5 people including a 13-year-old girl and a 9-month-old baby. The attacks had no coherent robbery motive. The perpetrators took small amounts of money and left expensive items behind. What they did leave behind was terror.

Investigations into the Brabant Massacres revealed connections to the Belgian stay-behind network SDRA8, to far-right political organizations, and to elements within the Belgian gendarmerie. Key witnesses died under suspicious circumstances. Evidence disappeared from police custody. The investigation was plagued by incompetence so systematic that it appeared deliberate. In 2018, former Belgian gendarmerie officer Madani Bouhouche — himself convicted of murder — claimed on his deathbed that the Brabant Massacres were connected to the Belgian stay-behind network and that the investigation had been deliberately obstructed from within the gendarmerie.

◆ The Andreotti Revelation

The existence of Operation Gladio was publicly revealed on October 24, 1990, by Italian Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti during testimony before a parliamentary commission investigating terrorism. Andreotti — himself a former defense minister and a figure deeply embedded in Italy's Cold War political establishment — confirmed that a secret army had existed in Italy since the 1950s, funded by the CIA and organized through NATO, with weapons caches hidden across the country. The revelation was not voluntary. Andreotti testified under pressure from Judge Casson's investigation, which had uncovered documentary evidence of the Gladio network's existence that could not be suppressed.

Andreotti's testimony sent shockwaves through European politics. The revelation that NATO member states had maintained secret armies — funded by a foreign intelligence service, equipped with military weapons, and implicated in terrorist attacks against their own citizens — triggered parliamentary investigations across Western Europe. In country after country, the existence of stay-behind networks was confirmed. In country after country, the same pattern emerged: secret armies, CIA funding, recruitment of former Nazis, weapons caches, and connections to acts of political violence.

The European Parliament condemned Gladio on November 22, 1990, passing a resolution that described the stay-behind armies as "a threat to democracy" and called on member states to conduct thorough investigations. But the resolution was non-binding, and most member states conducted investigations that were designed to reveal as little as possible. The CIA refused to cooperate. NATO refused to cooperate. The full scope of the stay-behind networks — their membership, their operations, their connections to political violence — has never been fully documented.

◆ The Parallel State

The most significant aspect of Operation Gladio is not the individual terrorist attacks it enabled but the structural precedent it established. Gladio created a parallel state — an institutional apparatus that existed outside democratic control, outside legal oversight, and outside public knowledge, and that operated on behalf of interests that were never articulated, let alone subjected to democratic accountability. This parallel state had its own military (the stay-behind armies), its own intelligence apparatus (the NATO clandestine committees), its own political networks (P2 in Italy, similar organizations elsewhere), and its own financial infrastructure (CIA funding channeled through NATO budgets that were immune to national audit).

The parallel state that Gladio created did not disappear when the program was officially terminated. It evolved. The institutional relationships — between intelligence services and neo-fascist groups, between military officers and Masonic lodges, between foreign funding and domestic terrorism — persisted beyond the Cold War framework that had justified their creation. The same networks that had carried out the strategy of tension in the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s adapted to new threats and new opportunities. The weapons changed. The targets changed. The ideology — the belief that democratic processes could be subverted in the name of national security — remained constant.

The connection to other programs documented on this site is structural rather than incidental. Like MKULTRA, Gladio represented the use of state power to conduct experiments on unwitting citizens — experiments in social engineering rather than mind control, but driven by the same institutional logic. Like COINTELPRO, Gladio targeted domestic political movements that the state considered threatening — not because they posed a genuine security risk, but because they represented an alternative to the existing power structure. And like the Pentagon's black budget, Gladio operated through financial structures that were specifically designed to prevent oversight, accountability, or public knowledge of what was being done in the citizens' name.

"Gladio did not defend democracy. It hollowed it out from the inside, leaving the shell of elections while filling the structure with weapons and secrets." — The Hidden Curriculum

The full truth of Operation Gladio has never been told. The archives remain classified. The surviving operatives remain silent. The institutional structures that enabled the strategy of tension continue to exist in modified form — adapted to the surveillance technologies and political dynamics of the twenty-first century, but built on the same foundation of secrecy, violence, and contempt for democratic governance. The secret armies were never disbanded. They were simply absorbed into the permanent infrastructure of the modern security state.
