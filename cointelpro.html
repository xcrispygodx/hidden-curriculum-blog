The Invisible War:
COINTELPRO and the FBI's War on Dissent

24 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

In August 1956, the Federal Bureau of Investigation launched a program that would, over the next fifteen years, systematically dismantle the constitutional rights of American citizens through a campaign of surveillance, infiltration, harassment, disinformation, and assassination. The program was called COINTELPRO — Counter Intelligence Program — and its stated purpose, in the language of the FBI's own internal memoranda, was to "expose, disrupt, misdirect, discredit, or otherwise neutralize" the activities of domestic organizations and individuals that the Bureau deemed subversive. The program was authorized by J. Edgar Hoover, directed by the FBI's Domestic Intelligence Division, and operated in complete secrecy for 15 years before its existence was revealed through an act of burglary.

COINTELPRO was not a single operation but a constellation of programs targeting different groups. COINTELPRO-Communist Party USA ran from 1956 to 1971. COINTELPRO-Socialist Workers Party ran from 1961 to 1969. COINTELPRO-Black Nationalist Hate Groups ran from 1967 to 1971. COINTELPRO-New Left ran from 1968 to 1971. COINTELPRO-White Hate Groups ran from 1964 to 1971. Each program had its own targets, its own methods, and its own body count. The common thread was the belief — shared by Hoover and the agents who carried out the operations — that any political movement that challenged the existing power structure was, by definition, a threat to national security, regardless of whether it advocated or employed violence.

The methods were comprehensive. The FBI used wiretaps, mail interception, tax audits, anonymous letters, forged documents, perjured testimony, psychological warfare, and — in the case of the Black Panther Party — assassination. The Bureau recruited informants to infiltrate organizations, planted agents provocateurs to incite violence that could be used to justify crackdowns, leaked damaging (and often fabricated) information to employers, landlords, family members, and the press, and coordinated with local police departments to ensure that targeted individuals were arrested on pretextual charges. The goal was not merely to monitor dissent but to destroy it — to make the cost of political opposition so high that no rational person would pay it.

◆ Martin Luther King Jr.: The Suicide Letter

The FBI's campaign against Martin Luther King Jr. represents one of the most sustained and vicious attacks on an individual by the US government in American history. The Bureau's interest in King began in 1962, when Hoover — who had already identified King as a dangerous radical based on his associations with Stanley Levison, a former Communist Party member — authorized wiretaps on King's home and office. The wiretaps were expanded in 1963, when Attorney General Robert Kennedy — under pressure from Hoover — approved electronic surveillance of King's hotel rooms during his travels.

The information gathered through these surveillance operations was used not to protect national security but to destroy a man. The FBI compiled dossiers on King's personal life, including details of extramarital affairs, and used this information to wage a psychological war designed to discredit him publicly and destroy him personally. The Bureau sent anonymous packages to King's wife, Coretta Scott King, containing recordings of his private conversations. It sent letters to newspaper editors, political leaders, and civil rights organizations suggesting that King was a communist agent. It pressured universities to cancel speaking engagements and threatened organizations that provided funding to the Southern Christian Leadership Conference.

The most infamous instrument of the FBI's campaign against King was the so-called "suicide letter" — an anonymous letter mailed to King on November 21, 1964, along with a package of recordings. The letter, written on FBI stationery but with no signature or return address, was composed by the FBI's Domestic Intelligence Division under the direction of William Sullivan, Hoover's assistant director. It was designed to read as if it had been written by a disillusioned supporter who had learned of King's personal conduct and was urging him to kill himself. The letter concluded with the words: "King, there is only one thing left for you to do. You know what it is. You have just 34 days in which to do it." The implication was unmistakable. The FBI — an agency of the United States government — was suggesting that Martin Luther King Jr. commit suicide.

King did not kill himself. He received the letter, recognized its origin, and continued his work. On April 4, 1968 — four years after the suicide letter — he was assassinated on the balcony of the Lorraine Motel in Memphis, Tennessee, by James Earl Ray. The extent of the FBI's involvement in King's assassination remains a subject of legitimate debate. A 1999 civil trial in Memphis, brought by the King family against Loyd Jowers and "other unknown co-conspirators," concluded that King had been killed as the result of a conspiracy involving elements of the US government — a verdict that the Department of Justice has never formally accepted or rejected.

◆ Fred Hampton: Assassination by Proxy

At 4:00 AM on December 4, 1969, a fourteen-member tactical unit of the Cook County State's Attorney's Office — acting on information provided by the FBI — raided a small apartment at 2337 West Monroe Street on Chicago's West Side. The apartment was the headquarters of the Illinois chapter of the Black Panther Party, and its most prominent resident was Fred Hampton, the 21-year-old chairman of the Illinois Panthers who had been identified by the FBI as "the new black messiah" with the potential to "unify, and electrify, the massive black community."

The tactical unit entered the apartment firing. Mark Clark, a Panther member on security duty, was killed immediately, shot through the heart. Hampton, who was in bed next to his eight-months-pregnant girlfriend, was shot twice in the head at close range as he lay unconscious. The other occupants of the apartment were beaten and arrested. The official account — that the Panthers had opened fire on the police first — was contradicted by the physical evidence, which showed a single bullet hole in the direction of the police from inside the apartment (fired by Clark as he died) and nearly 100 bullet holes fired into the apartment from outside.

The FBI's role in the raid was direct and documented. The Bureau had an informant inside the Black Panther Party — William O'Neal, who served as Hampton's bodyguard and security chief. On the night before the raid, O'Neal had prepared and served a dinner that was drugged with secobarbital, a powerful barbiturate. Hampton and the other occupants of the apartment were unconscious or severely sedated when the raid began. O'Neal had provided the FBI with a detailed floor plan of the apartment, which was passed to the tactical unit and used to plan the assault. The Bureau had effectively identified the target, drugged the victim, and provided the map for the killers.

O'Neal was paid $23,000 for his work as an FBI informant over the course of his infiltration of the Black Panther Party. He was provided with a new identity and relocated after the raid. In 1990, on the anniversary of Hampton's death, O'Neal committed suicide by running into traffic on the Eisenhower Expressway in Chicago. He had told a television interviewer the previous year that he had been "a tool" of the FBI and that his handler, Special Agent Roy Mitchell, had encouraged him to provoke violent confrontations within the Panther organization.

◆ "Prevent the Rise of a Messiah"

The FBI's internal memoranda reveal an institution that had defined its mission not as the enforcement of law but as the preservation of the existing social order. A 1968 FBI intelligence report on the Black Panther Party, written by the Bureau's Domestic Intelligence Division, contained a passage that has become one of the most quoted documents of the COINTELPRO era: the report stated that the Bureau's objective was to "prevent the rise of a 'messiah' who could unify and electrify the militant black nationalist movement." The report identified several potential "messiahs" — Malcolm X, Stokely Carmichael, Elijah Muhammad — and recommended that the Bureau take steps to prevent any one of them from achieving the level of influence necessary to unify the movement.

The "messiah" memo is significant not because it reveals a single conspiratorial thought but because it exposes the institutional logic that drove COINTELPRO. The FBI was not concerned with violence. It was not concerned with foreign influence. It was concerned with unity. The danger was not that black Americans would arm themselves — it was that they would organize themselves. The threat was not bombs but ballots. And the response was not to engage with legitimate grievances but to prevent the emergence of any leader capable of articulating them effectively.

This logic extended beyond the Black Panthers. COINTELPRO targeted the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, the Congress of Racial Equality, the American Indian Movement, the Young Lords, the Weather Underground, the Socialist Workers Party, and dozens of other organizations across the political spectrum. The common denominator was not ideology but effectiveness. Any organization that demonstrated the capacity to mobilize public opinion, disrupt the status quo, or challenge the legitimacy of existing institutions was targeted for neutralization.

◆ The Media Burglary: March 8, 1971

The existence of COINTELPRO was not revealed through congressional investigation, journalistic exposé, or government disclosure. It was revealed through an act of burglary. On the night of March 8, 1971 — while the nation was watching the Muhammad Ali vs. Joe Frazier "Fight of the Century" on closed-circuit television — a group of anti-war activists broke into the FBI's satellite office in Media, Pennsylvania, and stole every document they could carry. The burglars, who called themselves the Citizens' Commission to Investigate the FBI, took approximately 1,000 documents and mailed selected pages to journalists at the Washington Post and other newspapers.

The documents revealed the FBI's domestic surveillance operations in unprecedented detail. They showed that the Bureau had been monitoring political organizations, infiltrating peace groups, and compiling dossiers on American citizens engaged in lawful political activity. They introduced the public to the word "COINTELPRO" — an acronym that appeared on classified FBI memoranda authorizing specific operations against specific targets. The Washington Post, after verifying the authenticity of the documents, published a series of stories that exposed the Bureau's activities to public scrutiny for the first time.

The Media burglary was never solved. The burglars were never identified, arrested, or prosecuted. Their identities remained secret for 43 years, until 2014, when several members of the group revealed themselves publicly. The leaders — William Davidon, a physics professor at Haverford College, and John and Bonnie Raines — described the burglary as an act of civil disobedience motivated by the conviction that the FBI's surveillance operations were unconstitutional and that the public had a right to know about them. Their actions were, in the strictest sense, criminal. They broke into a federal building and stole government property. And by doing so, they exposed a criminal conspiracy that had been operating for 15 years with the full knowledge and authorization of the FBI's leadership.

◆ The Church Committee

The Media revelations, combined with the Watergate scandal and the publication of Seymour Hersh's 1974 New York Times investigation into CIA domestic operations, created the political conditions for a comprehensive investigation of US intelligence agencies. In January 1975, the Senate established the Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities — commonly known as the Church Committee, after its chairman, Senator Frank Church of Idaho.

The Church Committee's investigations, conducted over 15 months and covering 40 days of public hearings, produced 14 reports that documented abuses by the FBI, CIA, NSA, and IRS. The committee found that the FBI had conducted over 500,000 investigations of American citizens between 1960 and 1974. It documented the use of wiretaps, mail interception, and electronic surveillance against political dissidents, journalists, and members of Congress. It revealed that the CIA had conducted assassination plots against foreign leaders, including Fidel Castro, Patrice Lumumba, and Ngo Dinh Diem. And it found that the IRS had been used as a weapon against political enemies through selective audits and tax investigations.

The Church Committee's recommendations led to significant legislative reforms: the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978, which established a legal framework for intelligence surveillance; the Intelligence Oversight Act of 1980, which required congressional notification of covert operations; and the creation of the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, which provided permanent oversight of the intelligence community. But the reforms were limited in scope and weakly enforced. The intelligence agencies adapted to the new oversight regime by developing more sophisticated methods of concealment, by cultivating closer relationships with congressional overseers, and by expanding their use of private contractors who were not subject to the same reporting requirements as government employees.

◆ Jean Seberg: A Life Destroyed

The case of Jean Seberg illustrates the human cost of COINTELPRO with devastating clarity. Seberg was an American actress who achieved international fame as the star of Jean-Luc Godard's 1960 film "Breathless." She was also a vocal supporter of the civil rights movement and had made financial contributions to the Black Panther Party — activities that brought her to the FBI's attention during COINTELPRO-Black Nationalist Hate Groups.

In 1970, the FBI's Los Angeles field office proposed a plan to discredit Seberg through a disinformation campaign targeting her personal life. The plan was approved by FBI headquarters and carried out with the cooperation of Los Angeles-based journalists. The Bureau prepared a forged letter, purportedly written by a Black Panther Party member, that claimed Seberg was pregnant with the child of a Panther leader. The forged letter was sent to a Los Angeles gossip columnist, who published the story. The item was picked up by news outlets across the country.

Seberg, who was in fact pregnant, suffered the stress of public humiliation and gave birth prematurely on August 23, 1970. The baby, a girl named Nina, died two days later. Seberg was devastated by the loss and by the knowledge that her private life had been weaponized by the government. She attempted suicide multiple times in the years that followed. On August 30, 1979, she was found dead in the back seat of her car in Paris, parked near her apartment. She was 40 years old. The Paris police ruled her death a probable suicide by barbiturate overdose. Her husband, Romain Gary, later stated publicly that the FBI's campaign against her had been a direct cause of her mental deterioration and death.

The FBI acknowledged the Seberg operation in 1979, after the Los Angeles Times published details of the forgery. The Bureau characterized the operation as an "aberration" — a claim that is contradicted by the documentary record, which shows that similar disinformation campaigns were conducted against dozens of other targets. The difference was not the method but the outcome. In most cases, the targets survived. Jean Seberg did not.

◆ The American Indian Movement

The FBI's campaign against the American Indian Movement followed the same playbook used against the Black Panthers, the anti-war movement, and every other group targeted by COINTELPRO. AIM, founded in Minneapolis in 1968, advocated for treaty rights, tribal sovereignty, and the protection of Native American communities from police brutality and government neglect. Its leaders — including Russell Means, Dennis Banks, and Leonard Peltier — were identified by the FBI as threats to national security and subjected to intensive surveillance, infiltration, and legal harassment.

The FBI's involvement in the 1973 occupation of Wounded Knee on the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota was extensive and multifaceted. The Bureau provided intelligence support to the Bureau of Indian Affairs and the US Marshals Service during the 71-day standoff. It recruited and managed informants inside AIM. It coordinated with the tribal chairman, Dick Wilson, whose private militia — the Guardians of the Oglala Nation (GOON squad) — terrorized AIM supporters on the reservation. In the three years following the Wounded Knee occupation, more than 60 AIM members and supporters on Pine Ridge were murdered. The FBI investigated none of the killings.

Leonard Peltier was convicted in 1977 of the murder of two FBI agents during a 1975 shootout on the Pine Ridge Reservation. His trial was marked by coerced testimony, suppressed evidence, and prosecutorial misconduct that even the government's own appeals court acknowledged. Peltier's co-defendants were acquitted after a separate trial at which the same evidence was presented — but with a different judge, a different jury, and a prosecution that did not benefit from the same level of judicial cooperation. Peltier has been in federal prison since 1977. He has been denied parole repeatedly. He remains one of the longest-serving political prisoners in American history.

◆ The Machine That Never Stopped

The official narrative is that COINTELPRO ended in 1971, when the program was terminated following the Media burglary and subsequent investigations. This narrative is, at best, incomplete. The specific program called COINTELPRO was terminated. The institutional culture that created it — the culture of surveillance, infiltration, and the systematic destruction of political dissent — continued without interruption. The methods were refined, the targets updated, and the legal framework adjusted, but the fundamental orientation of the FBI toward domestic political movements remained unchanged.

The post-9/11 security state inherited COINTELPRO's DNA directly. The FBI's Joint Terrorism Task Forces, established after September 11, 2001, conducted surveillance operations against environmental groups, anti-war organizations, Occupy Wall Street protesters, Black Lives Matter activists, and Standing Rock water protectors that were indistinguishable in method from the COINTELPRO operations of the 1960s. The Bureau infiltrated mosques, monitored peace demonstrations, and compiled dossiers on American citizens engaged in lawful protest — all under the rubric of counterterrorism. The Patriot Act, passed 45 days after the September 11 attacks, provided a legal framework for surveillance operations that COINTELPRO had conducted without any legal framework at all.

The connection to MKULTRA is more than metaphorical. The CIA and FBI shared personnel, methods, and institutional culture throughout the Cold War. George Hunter White, the OSS officer who ran the CIA's Operation Midnight Climax, also conducted surveillance operations for the FBI. The pass-through foundations that funded MKULTRA research also funded FBI informant operations. The institutional boundary between foreign intelligence (the CIA's domain) and domestic intelligence (the FBI's domain) was always more porous than the legal framework suggested — and the post-9/11 security state has dissolved it entirely.

The defense establishment that COINTELPRO served — the same establishment that funded MKULTRA, recruited Nazi scientists through Operation Paperclip, and maintained secret armies through Operation Gladio — continues to operate with minimal oversight and maximum impunity. The budget has never been audited. The methods have never been repudiated. The victims have never been compensated. And the machine that was built to destroy dissent remains operational — waiting for the next leader who electrifies, the next movement that unifies, the next messiah who must be prevented from rising.
