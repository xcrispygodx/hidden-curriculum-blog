Creature from Jekyll Island:
The Private Bank That Owns Your Money

24 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

In November 1910, six men boarded a private railway car at the Hoboken Terminal in New Jersey under conditions of extreme secrecy. They traveled south for two days to the Jekyll Island Club, a private resort on a barrier island off the coast of Georgia that was accessible only by invitation and served as a retreat for some of the wealthiest families in America — the Rockefellers, the Morgans, the Vanderbilts, the Astors. The men who gathered there that weekend were among the most powerful financiers in the world, and they had come to draft the legislation that would create the central bank of the United States.

The six men were Senator Nelson Aldrich of Rhode Island — the Republican Senate whip and father-in-law of John D. Rockefeller Jr.; Paul Warburg of the banking house Kuhn, Loeb & Company — a German-born banker who had studied the central banks of Europe and would become the principal architect of the Federal Reserve System; Frank Vanderlip, president of the National City Bank of New York — the largest bank in America and the personal bank of the Rockefeller family; Henry Davison, senior partner of J.P. Morgan & Company; Charles Norton, president of the First National Bank of New York; and Benjamin Strong, vice president of Bankers Trust Company and future governor of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Each of these men represented a financial institution that stood to benefit enormously from the creation of a central bank. None of them represented the public interest.

The secrecy was not incidental. It was essential. The American public had rejected two previous attempts to establish a central bank — the First Bank of the United States (1791-1811) and the Second Bank of the United States (1816-1836) — on the grounds that a central bank concentrated too much financial power in too few hands. Andrew Jackson, who vetoed the recharter of the Second Bank in 1832, described it as "a den of vipers and thieves" and declared, "I killed the bank." The bankers who met at Jekyll Island understood that if the public knew who was drafting the legislation and whose interests it served, the bill would never pass. So they hid. Aldrich told his wife he was going on a duck-hunting trip. The men used first names only. The servants at the Jekyll Island Club were told that the guests were on a fishing expedition. And the document they produced — the Aldrich Plan — was presented to Congress as if it had been written by a senator rather than by the very bankers it was designed to benefit.

◆ The Federal Reserve Act: December 23, 1913

The Aldrich Plan, presented to Congress in January 1911, was defeated. The public and progressive politicians recognized it for what it was: a plan to give the banking industry control over the nation's money supply under the guise of reform. But the bankers were patient. They rebranded the plan, made cosmetic changes to address the most obvious objections, and reintroduced it as the Federal Reserve Act — sponsored by Carter Glass of Virginia and Robert Owen of Oklahoma, both Democrats, in a deliberate effort to distance the legislation from Aldrich's Republican associations.

The Federal Reserve Act was passed on December 23, 1913, by a vote of 298 to 60 in the House and by unanimous consent in the Senate — which was able to vote unanimously because most of the opponents had already left Washington for the Christmas recess. President Woodrow Wilson signed it into law the same day. The timing was not accidental. The supporters of the bill waited until the holiday season, when opposition was at its weakest, to bring it to a vote. The most consequential piece of financial legislation in American history — legislation that would affect the life of every American citizen for the next century and beyond — was passed while the country was shopping for Christmas presents.

The Federal Reserve Act created a system of 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks, each owned by the member banks in its district. The system was presented to the public as a decentralization of financial power — a network of regional banks that would prevent the concentration of monetary authority in New York. In practice, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York — located in the financial capital, controlled by the largest banks, and responsible for conducting open-market operations — dominated the system from the beginning. The Board of Governors, appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, was designed to provide public oversight. In practice, the Board was — and remains — drawn overwhelmingly from the banking industry it is supposed to regulate.

◆ The Fed Is Not Federal

The most important fact about the Federal Reserve System is also the most frequently obscured: the Federal Reserve is not a government agency. It is a private institution — or, more precisely, a hybrid entity that occupies the space between public and private in a way that is specifically designed to prevent accountability to either. The twelve Federal Reserve Banks are private corporations, owned by the member banks in their districts. The member banks hold stock in their regional Federal Reserve Bank, receive dividends on that stock, and elect six of the nine directors of each regional bank. The Board of Governors is a government agency. The Federal Open Market Committee — which makes the most consequential decisions about monetary policy — is composed of both Board members and regional bank presidents. The system is a chimera: public when it wants legitimacy, private when it wants freedom.

This structure has profound consequences. When the Federal Reserve makes a monetary policy decision — setting interest rates, buying or selling Treasury securities, expanding or contracting the money supply — it does so without meaningful public oversight, without congressional approval, and without judicial review. The Federal Open Market Committee meets in closed session. Its deliberations are classified. Its votes are recorded but its discussions are not disclosed for five years. The institution that controls the money supply of the world's largest economy operates with less transparency than the Central Intelligence Agency.

The Federal Reserve creates money through a mechanism that is, in its essence, a conjuring trick. When the government needs money, it issues Treasury bonds — IOUs promising to repay the borrowed amount with interest. The Federal Reserve buys these bonds with money that it creates out of nothing — by crediting the government's account with digits that exist only as entries in a computer system. The government then spends this money into the economy. The money circulates, generating economic activity. And then, over time, the government must repay the bonds — with interest — to the Federal Reserve. The interest is paid with tax revenue. The tax revenue comes from the labor of American citizens. The system, stripped to its essentials, is a mechanism for converting the labor of citizens into the profits of banks.

◆ Fractional Reserve: Money from Nothing

The Federal Reserve System operates on the principle of fractional reserve banking — a system in which banks are required to hold only a fraction of their deposits in reserve and are permitted to lend the rest. This means that when you deposit $1,000 in a bank, the bank keeps a fraction — currently 0% in the United States, as the reserve requirement was eliminated in March 2020 — and lends the rest. The borrower deposits the loan in another bank. That bank lends a fraction of that deposit. And so the cycle continues, multiplying the original deposit many times over. The $1,000 you deposited becomes $10,000 or more in the banking system — money that was created from nothing, backed by nothing, and exists only as debt.

This process — the creation of money through lending — is the primary mechanism by which the money supply expands. It is not controlled by Congress. It is not controlled by the Treasury. It is controlled by the private banks that make the lending decisions, with the Federal Reserve setting the parameters through reserve requirements (now zero), the discount rate, and open-market operations. The money supply of the United States — the total amount of money in circulation — is determined not by the elected government but by the lending decisions of private banks operating under the supervision of a privately owned central bank. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is the structure of the system as described in the Federal Reserve Act itself.

The implications of fractional reserve banking are staggering. Every dollar in the economy is created as debt — someone, somewhere, owes that dollar to a bank. The interest on that debt must be paid with money that was also created as debt. The system requires perpetual growth to function — because if new debt is not created to replace old debt as it is repaid, the money supply contracts and the economy collapses. This is why recessions are not accidents. They are features of a system that requires infinite growth on a finite planet — and that punishes any interruption in the expansion of debt with economic catastrophe.

◆ Crashes: 1929, 1987, 2008, 2020

The Federal Reserve was created to prevent financial panics. Its track record suggests that it has either failed catastrophically at this mission or that the panics are not failures but functions — events that serve the interests of the institutions that control the system by transferring wealth from the public to the financial elite during each crisis.

The stock market crash of October 1929 occurred after the Federal Reserve had spent the 1920s expanding the money supply through loose monetary policy, fueling a speculative bubble in stocks and real estate. When the Fed tightened policy in 1928-1929, the bubble burst. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 89% from its 1929 peak to its 1932 trough. The Great Depression that followed was the worst economic catastrophe in modern history — and it was enabled by the same institution that was supposed to prevent it. The banks that had fueled the bubble through reckless lending were bailed out. The depositors who had trusted those banks lost everything.

The pattern repeated. The Black Monday crash of October 19, 1987 — when the Dow fell 22.6% in a single day, the largest one-day percentage decline in history — followed a period of Fed tightening and rising interest rates. The 2008 financial crisis — triggered by the collapse of the subprime mortgage market, which had been inflated by years of low interest rates and lax regulation — required a $700 billion government bailout of the banking industry and a $4.5 trillion Federal Reserve program of quantitative easing (the creation of money to buy financial assets). The banks that had created the crisis through predatory lending, fraudulent securitization, and reckless risk management were rescued. The homeowners who had been targeted by those predatory loans lost their homes.

The COVID-19 crash of March 2020 followed the same pattern. The Federal Reserve cut interest rates to zero and launched an emergency quantitative easing program that would ultimately expand its balance sheet by $4.8 trillion — from $4.2 trillion in March 2020 to $8.9 trillion in early 2022. The money was used to buy Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities, pushing down interest rates and inflating asset prices. Stocks, real estate, and cryptocurrencies all surged to record highs. The beneficiaries were the owners of those assets — disproportionately wealthy, disproportionately white, and disproportionately connected to the financial system. The costs were borne by everyone else, through inflation that eroded wages, savings, and purchasing power.

◆ The 16th Amendment and the Income Tax

The Federal Reserve Act and the 16th Amendment to the Constitution — which authorized the federal income tax — were ratified in the same year: 1913. This is not a coincidence. It is a design feature. The income tax serves a dual purpose in the Federal Reserve system: it provides the revenue stream that services the national debt (which is owed, ultimately, to the Federal Reserve and its member banks), and it creates the legal and institutional apparatus for the collection and distribution of money throughout the economy.

The national debt of the United States — currently exceeding $34 trillion — is not money that the government owes to foreign countries or to its own citizens. The largest single holder of US government debt is the Federal Reserve System itself, which holds approximately $4.6 trillion in Treasury securities on its balance sheet. The interest on these securities is paid by the US Treasury — using tax revenue collected from American citizens — and remitted to the Federal Reserve, which in turn distributes a portion to its member banks as dividends. The system is, in its simplest terms, a mechanism for transferring wealth from taxpayers to bankers through the intermediation of government debt.

The historical parallel is instructive. In the 18th century, the Bank of England operated on the same principle: the Crown borrowed money from the Bank, the Bank created the money from nothing, and the Crown serviced the debt through taxation of its subjects. The American Revolution was fought, in part, to escape this system. The Founding Fathers — many of whom had studied the Bank of England and understood its mechanics — designed a constitution that gave Congress the power "to coin money" and "regulate the value thereof." The creation of the Federal Reserve in 1913 effectively transferred this power from Congress — the elected representatives of the people — to a privately owned banking system that is accountable to no one.

◆ JFK and Executive Order 11110

On June 4, 1963, President John F. Kennedy signed Executive Order 11110, which delegated to the Secretary of the Treasury the president's authority to issue silver certificates — paper currency backed by silver held in the US Treasury. The order effectively authorized the Treasury to create money directly, bypassing the Federal Reserve System. Under Kennedy's order, the Treasury issued $4.2 billion in silver certificates in denominations of $2 and $5 — currency that was backed by silver rather than by the Federal Reserve's credit system.

Five months later, on November 22, 1963, Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas, Texas. The connection between Executive Order 11110 and the assassination is speculative — there is no direct evidence that Kennedy's challenge to the Federal Reserve was a motive for his murder. But the timing is notable, and the order was never implemented after Kennedy's death. Lyndon Johnson, who assumed the presidency within hours of the assassination, halted the issuance of silver certificates and returned monetary policy to the Federal Reserve. The silver certificates that had been issued under Kennedy's order were gradually withdrawn from circulation.

Whatever the true relationship between Executive Order 11110 and Kennedy's death, the episode illustrates a pattern that this site documents repeatedly: American presidents who challenge the fundamental structures of power — whether the intelligence apparatus, the military-industrial complex, or the banking system — tend to meet violent ends or experience political destruction. Andrew Jackson survived an assassination attempt after destroying the Second Bank. Abraham Lincoln was assassinated after issuing Greenbacks — interest-free currency created by the Treasury rather than borrowed from banks. William McKinley was assassinated after opposing the gold standard. The pattern does not prove conspiracy. But it establishes a consistent correlation between challenging financial power and paying with your life.

◆ The Bank for International Settlements

The Federal Reserve does not operate in isolation. It is part of a global network of central banks coordinated through the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) — the "central bank of central banks" — headquartered in Basel, Switzerland. The BIS was established in 1930 to facilitate the payment of German reparations after World War I, but its role has expanded enormously in the decades since. Today, the BIS coordinates monetary policy among its 63 member central banks, sets capital requirements for commercial banks through the Basel Accords, and serves as a forum for the informal agreements that govern the global financial system.

The BIS enjoys a unique legal status: it is immune from national law. The organization's founding charter grants it inviolability for its premises, archives, and assets. BIS employees enjoy diplomatic immunity. The organization is not subject to the jurisdiction of any national court. Its meetings are closed. Its records are classified. The institution that effectively governs the global financial system operates with less transparency and less accountability than any national government on Earth.
