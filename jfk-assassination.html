Dallas and the Powers He Challenged:
The JFK Assassination

26 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

At 12:30 PM on November 22, 1963, President John Fitzgerald Kennedy was shot and killed as his motorcade passed through Dealey Plaza in Dallas, Texas. The shots came from above and behind — at least according to the official account — from the sixth floor of the Texas School Book Depository, where a former Marine named Lee Harvey Oswald had positioned himself with a 6.5mm Mannlicher-Carcano rifle. Kennedy was struck twice: once in the upper back, and once in the head. The head wound was fatal. He was pronounced dead at Parkland Memorial Hospital at 1:00 PM, 30 minutes after the shooting. He was 46 years old.

The assassination of John F. Kennedy is the central event of the American twentieth century — not because Kennedy was the greatest president, or the most beloved, or even the most consequential, but because his murder marked the moment when the possibility of democratic reform in the United States was foreclosed. Kennedy was not killed by a lone gunman acting on impulse. He was killed because he had, in the thousand days of his presidency, challenged the most powerful institutions in the American state — and those institutions responded in the way that powerful institutions always respond when their authority is genuinely threatened: with violence.

This article does not attempt to solve the Kennedy assassination. The evidence has been too thoroughly contaminated, the witnesses too conveniently dead, and the institutional machinery of concealment too effectively deployed for any definitive resolution. What this article does is document the powers that Kennedy challenged and the extraordinary coincidence — if it is a coincidence — that the removal of the one man who threatened each of these structures simultaneously was accomplished by a single, unaffiliated, psychologically unstable individual who was himself killed two days later before he could be examined by a psychiatrist.

◆ The Warren Commission vs. Reality

The Warren Commission, established by President Lyndon Johnson on November 29, 1963 — one week after the assassination — concluded in September 1964 that Lee Harvey Oswald had acted alone in killing Kennedy, and that Jack Ruby had acted alone in killing Oswald. The Commission found no evidence of conspiracy, domestic or foreign. It found no evidence of CIA involvement, FBI involvement, or organized crime involvement. It found that Oswald had fired three shots from the sixth floor of the Texas School Book Depository in approximately 8.3 seconds, using a bolt-action rifle, with two of the three shots striking the president.

The problems with the Warren Commission's conclusions are numerous and well-documented. The "single bullet theory" — the claim that one bullet caused seven wounds in both Kennedy and Governor John Connally, who was sitting in front of the president — defies the laws of physics as understood by every independent ballistic expert who has examined it. The bullet, designated CE 399 and known as the "magic bullet," was found on a stretcher at Parkland Hospital in nearly pristine condition — despite allegedly having shattered Connally's rib and wrist after passing through Kennedy's neck. The trajectory required to produce all seven wounds with a single bullet from the sixth-floor window is geometrically impossible given the relative seating positions of Kennedy and Connally.

The Commission's investigation was compromised from the beginning. Allen Dulles — the former Director of Central Intelligence whom Kennedy had fired after the Bay of Pigs — was appointed as a member of the Commission. The CIA, which should have been a primary subject of the investigation, was effectively allowed to investigate itself. The FBI, which had been monitoring Oswald before the assassination and had failed to flag him as a threat, provided the Commission with its primary investigative support. The institutions with the greatest motive to suppress evidence were the same institutions responsible for gathering it.

◆ Kennedy vs. the CIA

The conflict between Kennedy and the Central Intelligence Agency was not a policy disagreement. It was a war. It began with the Bay of Pigs invasion in April 1961 — a CIA-planned operation to overthrow Fidel Castro that had been conceived under Eisenhower and inherited by Kennedy. The plan called for a CIA-trained force of 1,400 Cuban exiles to land at the Bay of Pigs on Cuba's southern coast, where they would trigger a popular uprising against Castro. Kennedy approved the operation but refused to provide the US air support that the CIA considered essential for its success. The invasion failed. The exiles were captured or killed. Castro remained in power. And the CIA blamed Kennedy.

The Bay of Pigs was a catastrophe for both Kennedy and the CIA, but their responses diverged fundamentally. Kennedy took responsibility publicly and was humiliated. Privately, he was furious — not at the operation's failure, but at the CIA for having presented him with a plan that was designed to fail without direct US military intervention, effectively forcing him to choose between escalation and defeat. Kennedy told his aides that he wanted to "splinter the CIA into a thousand pieces and scatter it to the winds." He fired Allen Dulles, the Director of Central Intelligence. He fired Deputy Director Charles Cabell. He fired Deputy Director for Plans Richard Bissell — the architect of the Bay of Pigs operation and the man who had overseen MKULTRA.

But Kennedy went further than personnel changes. In NSAM 57, issued in June 1961, he transferred covert operations authority from the CIA to the Department of Defense — effectively stripping the agency of its primary operational function. In 1962-1963, he pursued secret peace negotiations with Castro through back channels, bypassing the CIA entirely. He rejected the CIA's recommendations for military intervention in Laos. He refused to authorize the use of tactical nuclear weapons during the Cuban Missile Crisis — overriding the Joint Chiefs of Staff, who had unanimously recommended an invasion of Cuba. By the fall of 1963, Kennedy had made enemies of every institution that held real power in the American national security state.

◆ Kennedy vs. the Federal Reserve

Kennedy's challenge to the Federal Reserve System is the least discussed aspect of his presidency and, for some analysts, the most significant. On June 4, 1963, Kennedy signed Executive Order 11110, which delegated to the Secretary of the Treasury the president's authority under the Thomas Amendment to the Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1934 to issue silver certificates — currency backed by silver held in the US Treasury. Under this order, the Treasury began issuing silver certificates in denominations of $2 and $5, with a total issuance of approximately $4.2 billion.

The significance of Executive Order 11110 is contested. Skeptics argue that it was a routine administrative action that merely consolidated existing presidential authority and did not represent a challenge to the Federal Reserve. Proponents argue that it was the first step in a plan to gradually transfer the money-creation power from the Federal Reserve back to the Treasury — effectively undoing the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 and returning to a system in which the government created money directly, without borrowing from a privately owned central bank.

What is not contested is the sequence of events. Kennedy signed Executive Order 11110 in June 1963. He was killed in November 1963. Lyndon Johnson, who assumed the presidency, halted the issuance of silver certificates and reversed Kennedy's monetary policy. The silver certificates were gradually withdrawn from circulation. The Federal Reserve's monopoly on money creation was preserved. Whether or not Kennedy's monetary policy was a motive for his assassination, the policy died with him — and no subsequent president has attempted anything similar.

◆ Kennedy vs. the Military-Industrial Complex

On January 17, 1961, three days before Kennedy's inauguration, President Dwight Eisenhower delivered his farewell address — a speech that has become one of the most quoted presidential addresses in American history. Eisenhower warned against "the acquisition of unwarranted influence, whether sought or unsought, by the military-industrial complex." He cautioned that "the potential for the disastrous rise of misplaced power exists and will persist." He urged the American people to remain vigilant against the "danger that public policy could itself become captive of a scientific-technological elite."

Kennedy took Eisenhower's warning seriously. In his first year in office, he rejected the Joint Chiefs' recommendation for a preemptive nuclear strike against the Soviet Union during the Berlin Crisis. In his second year, he rejected the military's recommendation for direct US military intervention in Cuba during the Missile Crisis. In his third year, he began the process of withdrawing American military advisors from Vietnam — a process that, if completed, would have ended the military-industrial complex's most profitable enterprise of the postwar era.

On October 11, 1963, Kennedy signed National Security Action Memorandum 263 (NSAM 263), which ordered the withdrawal of 1,000 US military personnel from Vietnam by the end of 1963 and the complete withdrawal of all US forces by 1965. The order was opposed by the Joint Chiefs of Staff, by the military commanders in Vietnam, and by the defense contractors who stood to lose billions in contracts if the war ended before it began. Five weeks later, Kennedy was dead. Three days after the assassination, Lyndon Johnson signed NSAM 273, reversing Kennedy's withdrawal order and beginning the escalation that would ultimately deploy 536,000 American troops to Vietnam and kill 58,000 of them.

◆ Kennedy vs. Vietnam

The Vietnam question is central to the Kennedy assassination because it represents the largest financial interest at stake. In 1963, the military-industrial complex's investment in the Vietnam War was still prospective — the war had not yet become the trillion-dollar enterprise it would become under Johnson and Nixon. But the trajectory was clear. The Defense Department had been planning for a major Southeast Asian conflict since the late 1950s. The contracts were being drawn. The bases were being built. The advisors were being deployed. And Kennedy was, by all evidence, planning to end it before it started.

The significance of NSAM 263 cannot be overstated. The memorandum represented not just a policy decision but a fundamental challenge to the institutional interests that controlled American foreign policy. The military establishment had been planning for a ground war in Vietnam. The intelligence establishment had been running covert operations in Vietnam since the 1950s. The defense contractors had been developing weapons systems specifically designed for Southeast Asian conditions. Kennedy's withdrawal order threatened all of these investments — and the people and institutions that had made them.

The men who opposed Kennedy's Vietnam policy were not marginal figures. They included General Curtis LeMay, the Air Force Chief of Staff who had advocated for nuclear bombing of Cuba during the Missile Crisis. They included General Maxwell Taylor, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs. They included the entire command structure of the Military Assistance Command, Vietnam (MACV). And they included the civilian defense establishment — the Pentagon officials, defense contractors, and think-tank analysts who had spent a decade building the infrastructure for a war they considered inevitable and profitable. Kennedy was not merely opposing a policy. He was opposing an entire institutional ecosystem.

◆ Kennedy vs. Israel's Nuclear Program

The least discussed conflict of the Kennedy presidency was his confrontation with Israel over its nuclear weapons program. Kennedy was the first and only American president to actively oppose Israel's development of nuclear weapons. He insisted on American inspection of the Dimona nuclear reactor in the Negev Desert, which US intelligence believed was being used to produce weapons-grade plutonium. Israeli Prime Minister David Ben-Gurion and his successor Levi Eshkol repeatedly refused to allow American inspectors access to the facility, and Kennedy responded with escalating diplomatic pressure.

In a series of letters to Eshkol in 1963, Kennedy made clear that American support for Israel — including the sale of Hawk anti-aircraft missiles and the provision of diplomatic cover at the United Nations — was contingent on Israel's acceptance of American inspection of Dimona. Kennedy's final letter, sent in July 1963, stated that the United States commitment to Israel's security "could be seriously jeopardized" if Israel refused to allow inspections. The language was the strongest any American president had ever used with Israel. No subsequent president has repeated it.

After Kennedy's assassination, Lyndon Johnson abandoned the inspection demand and established the policy of "nuclear ambiguity" that has governed US-Israel relations on the nuclear question ever since. Israel has never officially acknowledged possessing nuclear weapons, despite the consensus among intelligence analysts that it has maintained an arsenal of 80 to 400 warheads since the late 1960s. The Dimona facility has never been inspected by the International Atomic Energy Agency or any other international body. Kennedy's insistence on accountability was buried with him.

◆ Jack Ruby: The Silencing

Lee Harvey Oswald was shot and killed on November 24, 1963, two days after the assassination of Kennedy, by Jack Ruby — a Dallas nightclub owner with documented connections to organized crime and suspected connections to intelligence agencies. Ruby shot Oswald in the basement of the Dallas Police Headquarters, in front of 70 police officers and a live national television audience, as Oswald was being transferred to the county jail. The shooting was broadcast live on NBC. The most important witness in American criminal history was silenced on camera.

Ruby's background defies the "lone avenger" narrative that the Warren Commission constructed. He had extensive connections to organized crime figures in Chicago, New Orleans, and Havana. He had been involved in gun-running to Cuba. He had visited Cuba on multiple occasions during the period when the CIA was running anti-Castro operations. He had connections to Santos Trafficante, the Tampa mob boss who had been targeted by Castro's revolution and who had been recruited by the CIA in 1960 for assassination plots against Castro. He had connections to Carlos Marcello, the New Orleans mob boss who had been deported by Robert Kennedy's Justice Department and who had publicly threatened to assassinate the president.

The Warren Commission dismissed Ruby's mob connections as irrelevant and concluded that Ruby had acted spontaneously — overcome by grief and a desire to spare Jacqueline Kennedy the ordeal of a trial. This explanation requires the acceptance of multiple improbable propositions: that Ruby, a man with deep criminal connections, just happened to be in the police basement at the exact moment Oswald was being transferred; that he brought a gun to a location guarded by dozens of police officers without being searched; that he acted alone and without foreknowledge; and that his connections to organized crime and intelligence were coincidental to his presence at the scene of the most important crime of the twentieth century.

◆ The Zapruder Film and the Grassy Knoll

Abraham Zapruder, a dressmaker who worked in the Dal-Tex Building overlooking Dealey Plaza, filmed the assassination with a Bell & Howell Zoomatic Director Series Model 414 PD home movie camera. The 26.6-second film — 486 frames shot at 18.3 frames per second — is the most important piece of evidence in the case. It shows Kennedy's motorcade turning from Houston Street onto Elm Street, the moments before the shots, and the fatal head wound in graphic detail.

The Zapruder film contradicts the Warren Commission's single-gunman conclusion in several critical respects. Frame 313 shows the fatal head shot — and the direction of Kennedy's head movement is backward and to the left, consistent with a shot from the right front (the "grassy knoll" area) rather than from the rear (the Texas School Book Depository). The Warren Commission concluded that the backward movement was caused by a "neuromuscular reaction" — an explanation that has been rejected by virtually every independent forensic pathologist who has examined the film. The Commission's own forensic expert, Dr. Alfred Olivier, testified that the movement was consistent with a shot from the rear only if one assumed a "jet effect" from the exit wound — an assumption that has been experimentally disproven.

The grassy knoll — a small hill and picket fence area to the right front of the presidential limousine — has been the focus of assassination research since the day of the shooting. Multiple witnesses reported hearing shots from the knoll area. Several witnesses reported seeing smoke or a puff of smoke behind the picket fence. Railroad worker S.M. Holland, who was standing on the overpass above the plaza, signed an affidavit stating that he saw a shot come from behind the knoll fence. Lee Bowers, who was in a railroad tower behind the knoll, testified that he saw two men behind the fence in the minutes before the shooting and that one of them made a suspicious movement at the time of the shots. Bowers died in a single-car accident in 1966. Holland was never called before the Warren Commission.

◆ The George H.W. Bush Question

The presence of George Herbert Walker Bush in the orbit of the Kennedy assassination is one of the most tantalizing and least resolved threads of the case. Bush, who would become the 41st President of the United States, was in Dallas on November 22, 1963. He was the 38-year-old president of Zapata Petroleum, an oil company with documented connections to CIA operations in Cuba. His alibi for the day of the assassination — that he was in Tyler, Texas, 100 miles from Dallas, attending a luncheon of the American Association of Oilwell Drilling Contractors — has been questioned by researchers who have noted inconsistencies in his account and the difficulty of confirming his presence in Tyler at the time of the shooting.

On November 29, 1963 — one week after the assassination — FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover wrote a memorandum documenting a briefing he had given to "George Bush of the Central Intelligence Agency" regarding the assassination and the FBI's concern about public reaction to the case. The George H.W. Bush Presidential Library has acknowledged that this memorandum exists but has stated that it refers to a different George Bush — a George William Bush who was a CIA employee at the time. This explanation requires the acceptance of the proposition that J. Edgar Hoover, the most meticulous bureaucrat in the history of American government, confused two different George Bushes — one a prominent Texas oilman with CIA connections, and the other a relatively junior CIA employee — in a memorandum about the assassination of the president.

George H.W. Bush's subsequent career is itself significant. He served as a congressman, ambassador to the United Nations, chairman of the Republican National Committee, director of the Central Intelligence Agency (1976-1977), vice president, and president. As CIA director, he presided over the agency during a period when it was under intense congressional scrutiny following the Church Committee investigations — and he successfully managed the agency through that crisis, preserving its operational capabilities while making the minimal concessions necessary to satisfy congressional oversight. The man whose alibi for November 22, 1963, was never adequately verified went on to lead the agency that had the strongest institutional motive for Kennedy's removal.

◆ The Pattern

The Kennedy assassination is not an isolated event. It is the most visible instance of a pattern that runs through the history of the American republic: the destruction of political leaders who challenge the fundamental structures of power. Abraham Lincoln, who issued Greenbacks that bypassed the banking system, was assassinated. James Garfield, who opposed the gold standard and advocated for monetary reform, was assassinated. William McKinley, who opposed the gold standard and was succeeded by the banker-friendly Theodore Roosevelt, was assassinated. John Kennedy, who challenged the CIA, the Federal Reserve, the military-industrial complex, and the Israeli nuclear program, was assassinated. Robert Kennedy, who was running for president on a platform that included reopening the investigation into his brother's murder, was assassinated. Martin Luther King Jr., who was expanding his civil rights campaign into an anti-war and anti-poverty movement that threatened the same structures, was assassinated.

The probability that these events are unrelated — that each was the act of a lone, mentally unstable individual who happened to choose as his target the one person most threatening to the existing power structure — is vanishingly small. The probability that they are connected — that a pattern of political assassination has been employed as a tool of institutional self-preservation — is, at the very least, worth considering. The evidence is incomplete. The witnesses are dead. The archives are sealed. But the pattern persists.

The connections to other articles on this site are structural. The Federal Reserve that Kennedy challenged has never been challenged since. The CIA that ran MKULTRA was the same agency whose leadership Kennedy destroyed after the Bay of Pigs. The military-industrial complex that Eisenhower warned about and Kennedy opposed has grown into a $900 billion annual enterprise that has never been audited. The Nazi scientists that the CIA recruited built the weapons systems and space programs that the military-industrial complex sold to the Pentagon at whatever price it chose. And the COINTELPRO program that the FBI ran against domestic dissenters shared methods, personnel, and institutional culture with the intelligence apparatus that Kennedy had tried to dismantle.

The Kennedy assassination was not a single event. It was a hinge — the moment when the trajectory of American democracy shifted from reform to consolidation, from openness to secrecy, from democratic accountability to institutional immunity.
