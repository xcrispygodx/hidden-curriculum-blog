The Maxwell-Epstein Web:
Textbooks, Spies, and the Geometry of Control

22 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

After World War II, the United States government did something unprecedented: it classified mathematics. Not weapons. Not intelligence reports. The equations themselves. Encryption algorithms were placed on the United States Munitions List alongside bombs and flamethrowers. Publishing a prime number could make you an international arms trafficker. This is not metaphor. This is documented legal history.

But the classification of mathematics was only the beginning. What followed was a multi-generational, multi-national operation to control who had access to mathematical knowledge, what got taught in schools, what got published in journals, and who got recruited into the machine. The thread runs from Los Alamos to a textbook publisher, from a Mossad asset to a convicted sex offender, from nuclear weapons labs in the New Mexico desert to classrooms across America.

I. The Mathematicians of New Mexico

In 1943, the U.S. Army selected a mesa in the Jemez Mountains of northern New Mexico for the most secret scientific project in history. Los Alamos National Laboratory — "Project Y" — became the crucible where the world's greatest mathematicians and physicists were concentrated to build the atomic bomb.

The names are legendary: John von Neumann, the Hungarian prodigy who invented game theory, the stored-program computer, and the implosion lens for the plutonium bomb. Stanislaw Ulam, the Polish mathematician who co-invented the Monte Carlo method and the hydrogen bomb design. Richard Feynman, Edward Teller, Enrico Fermi, Hans Bethe — an assembly of intellect the world had never seen before and may never see again.

These men created entirely new mathematical disciplines that were initially classified before entering mainstream academia:

Classified Mathematics That Entered Academia

  • Monte Carlo Simulation — Classified until 1949. Created by Ulam and von Neumann for nuclear weapons calculations.
  • Game Theory — Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944. Born from strategic weapons analysis.
  • Operations Research — Emerged from wartime logistics and targeting analysis.
  • Linear Programming — Developed for military resource allocation.
  • Modern Computer Architecture — The von Neumann architecture, designed for bomb calculations, became the basis of every computer on Earth.
  • Cybernetics — Norbert Wiener's feedback theory, funded by military contracts.

The Monte Carlo method is a perfect case study. Ulam conceived it while recovering from illness and playing solitaire — could he calculate the probability of winning by randomly simulating games rather than analytically solving every possibility? The answer led to a technique that could simulate nuclear chain reactions. It was classified because it was essential to weapons design. It wasn't published openly until 1949, and only after fierce internal debate about what mathematics should be allowed to exist in the public sphere.

"I am thinking about something much more important than bombs; I am thinking about computers." — John von Neumann, during the Manhattan Project

In 1984, a group of Los Alamos scientists — led by George Cowan, a physical chemist who had spent 35 years at the laboratory — founded the Santa Fe Institute (SFI) in nearby Santa Fe, New Mexico. SFI became the world's premier center for complexity science, chaos theory, and complex adaptive systems. Nobel laureate Murray Gell-Mann (quark theory) was among its founding figures. The institute was, in every meaningful sense, an extension of Los Alamos into civilian academia.

Remember the Santa Fe Institute. It will appear again.

II. Cryptography as a Weapon

Starting November 17, 1954, the U.S. government placed encryption technology on the United States Munitions List. This wasn't an abstraction. The legal consequence was that mathematical algorithms — the RSA encryption system, the Diffie-Hellman key exchange, the very mathematics that would later secure every online transaction on Earth — were classified as weapons of war.

In the 1990s, the "Crypto Wars" reached their peak:

The Crypto Wars: When Math Was a Munition

  • Phil Zimmermann was criminally investigated for 3 years (1993-1996) for distributing PGP encryption software.
  • Daniel Bernstein, a UC Berkeley math Ph.D. student, was told he needed to register as an arms dealer to publish his encryption algorithm "Snuffle."
  • Activists printed RSA source code on T-shirts — technically making the shirts export-restricted munitions under ITAR.
  • MIT Press published the full PGP source code as a paperback book, exploiting the loophole that books are protected speech.
  • Netscape shipped intentionally weakened 40-bit encryption internationally — later broken by the FREAK attack in 2015.

The Bernstein v. United States case (1995-1999) established, after years of litigation, that mathematical code is protected speech under the First Amendment. But the framework for classifying mathematics never fully went away. The Wassenaar Arrangement, a multilateral export control regime with 42 participating states, still classifies certain cryptographic items alongside conventional arms on its Munitions List today.

III. Robert Maxwell: The Publisher Who Was a Spy

Robert Maxwell was born Jan Ludvik Hoch in 1923 in Czechoslovakia. He escaped the Nazis, fought with British forces, was decorated by Field Marshal Montgomery, and changed his name five times. By the end of his life he was one of the most powerful media moguls in the world — and, according to multiple independent investigations, an intelligence asset operating for multiple nations.

His daughter Ghislaine told the FBI in a deposition that her father was an intelligence asset for the British "and others." British Foreign Office files, partially declassified, described his "questionable activities" and labeled him "a thoroughly bad character and almost certainly financed by Russia."

By the 1980s, Maxwell had assembled one of the most consequential publishing empires in history:

Robert Maxwell's Publishing Empire

  • Pergamon Press — Dominated scientific/academic journals worldwide. Controlled which research got published and which didn't. Maxwell effectively shaped the modern peer review system.
  • Macmillan Inc. — Acquired in 1988 for $2.5 billion. One of the largest textbook publishers in American schools.
  • Mirror Group Newspapers — Major UK media outlet.
  • Information on Demand — A U.S.-based company quietly acquired by Pergamon, used as a vehicle for intelligence operations.

In 1989, Macmillan merged with McGraw-Hill to form Macmillan/McGraw-Hill, which became one of the dominant K-12 textbook publishers in the United States. Millions of American schoolchildren learned from textbooks connected to an alleged multi-agency intelligence asset.

IV. The PROMIS Operation: Maxwell in New Mexico

In 1984, Maxwell targeted the two most sensitive nuclear weapons facilities in the United States — both in New Mexico — for a software sale that federal investigators would later allege was an intelligence operation.

The software was PROMIS (Prosecutor's Management Information System), developed by Inslaw Inc. It could simultaneously query and integrate information from multiple incompatible databases — a surveillance tool of extraordinary potential in the pre-internet era. A federal bankruptcy court found that the DOJ had obtained enhanced versions of PROMIS through "trickery, fraud and deceit."

According to FBI counterintelligence records and investigative accounts compiled by Gordon Thomas and Martin Dillon in Robert Maxwell, Israel's Superspy (2002):

  • Israeli intelligence, under Mossad operations chief Rafi Eitan (also the handler of Jonathan Pollard, convicted of passing U.S. nuclear secrets to Israel), modified PROMIS with a hidden backdoor
  • Maxwell was tasked with selling the compromised version to governments and institutions worldwide
  • He targeted Sandia National Laboratories (Albuquerque) and Los Alamos National Laboratory
  • Two Sandia employees approached the FBI's New Mexico field office, reporting that Maxwell's company had acquired an NSA database and was attempting to sell surveillance software to the labs
  • The FBI opened a counterintelligence investigation — then FBI headquarters ordered it shut down in August 1984
  • In February 1985, Maxwell signed the contract for the PROMIS installation at Sandia, listing himself as President and CEO of Information on Demand

The investigation was closed. The software was installed. Every query made through PROMIS at Sandia potentially funneled data back through the backdoor. Maxwell's daughter Christine was subsequently installed as president and CEO of Information on Demand.

Maxwell's FBI file has grown progressively more classified over time — more heavily redacted in 2013 than in 1994, and more heavily redacted still in 2017. This is the inverse of what normally happens with aging intelligence files. — MuckRock FOIA Analysis, 2017
