◆ The Bretton Woods Collapse (1971)

On August 15, 1971, Richard Nixon stood before the American people and announced that the United States would no longer redeem dollars for gold. The Bretton Woods system — established in 1944 to anchor the world's currencies to the dollar, which was in turn anchored to gold — was dead. The dollar was now naked fiat currency, backed by nothing but the full faith and credit of the United States government.

The crisis had been building for years. The Vietnam War and domestic spending programs had flooded the world with dollars. Foreign governments, led by France under Charles de Gaulle, began converting their dollar holdings into gold. Fort Knox was emptying. Nixon's solution was not to stop spending. It was to stop honoring the promise.

The consequences were immediate. Without the gold anchor, the dollar began to float — and to fall. Inflation surged. By 1974, consumer price inflation had reached 12.3 percent. The dollar had lost its claim to intrinsic value. The United States needed a new reason for the world to hold dollars. It needed something that every nation on Earth would be forced to buy — something that could only be purchased in American currency.

It needed oil.

The artificial demand for the dollar was permanent, structural, and global.
