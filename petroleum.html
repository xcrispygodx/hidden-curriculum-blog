The Black Mirror:
What Plastics Actually Are and How They're Made

12 MIN READ • MARCH 2026 • THE HIDDEN CURRICULUM

Plastics are everywhere. They are in your blood. They are in your brain. They are in your placenta. As of February 2025, a study published in Nature Medicine confirmed that 0.5% of human brain tissue is now plastic by weight. In patients with dementia, that number rises to 5–7 times higher. You are not just surrounded by plastic — you are becoming plastic.

This is not a metaphor. This is not hyperbole. This is the measurable, quantifiable reality of a material that was engineered from petroleum waste, marketed as progress, and now saturates every living system on Earth. To understand how we got here, you have to understand what petroleum actually is, what plastic actually is, and why neither was ever designed to go away.

◆ What Petroleum Actually Is

The phrase "fossil fuel" is so deeply embedded in public consciousness that most people never question it. The story is simple: ancient plants and dinosaurs died, were buried, compressed over millions of years, and became oil. It is intuitive, it is clean, and it supports the central premise of modern energy economics — that petroleum is scarce, finite, and irreplaceable.

There is an alternative theory that has been suppressed for over a century.

In 1877, Dmitri Mendeleev — the chemist who created the periodic table — proposed that petroleum forms not from biological matter but from deep geological processes involving iron carbides and water at extreme temperatures and pressures. In 1951, Soviet geologist Nikolai Kudryavtsev formalized this as "Kudryavtsev's Rule," which observed that oil deposits are found at every depth, including far below the sedimentary layers where fossils exist, and that oil fields frequently refill themselves.

⚠ The Abiotic Oil Evidence

  • Mendeleev proposed abiotic petroleum formation in 1877, challenging biotic theory decades before it became dominant.
  • Kudryavtsev's Rule (1951) observed that oil exists at depths and in geological formations incompatible with biological origin.
  • Thomas Gold's "Deep Hot Biosphere" theory (1999) proposed petroleum forms from primordial carbon deep within the Earth's mantle.
  • Eugene Island 330 — a Gulf of Mexico oil field — was depleting normally until it began refilling itself in the 1990s, with oil migrating from a deep source below.
  • Soviet scientists successfully drilled abiotic oil wells in the Dnieper-Donets Basin (1990s) in crystalline basement rock with no biological source material.
  • The "fossil fuel" label may itself be a manufactured scarcity narrative designed to justify pricing and control.

The implications are significant. If petroleum is abiotic — if it is continuously generated by geological processes deep within the Earth — then "peak oil" is not a geological inevitability but an economic construction. The scarcity that drives pricing, geopolitical conflict, and the entire architecture of modern industry may be partially or entirely artificial.

This does not mean petroleum is harmless. It means the narrative around it has been shaped to serve specific interests. And those interests extend far beyond fuel.

◆ The Refining Process

Crude oil is not useful in its raw form. It must be refined — separated into its component fractions through fractional distillation. The crude oil is heated to approximately 350°C and fed into a distillation column, where different hydrocarbons separate based on their boiling points:

Fraction Boiling Range Carbon Atoms Primary Use
Refinery Gas < 40°C 1–4 Heating, LPG
Gasoline (Petrol) 40–180°C 5–12 Vehicle fuel
Naphtha 60–170°C 5–12 Plastic feedstock (key)
Kerosene 175–325°C 12–16 Jet fuel, heating
Diesel 250–350°C 15–20 Diesel fuel
Lubricating Oil 300–370°C 20–50 Machine lubricants
Bitumen > 350°C 70+ Road surfacing

Naphtha is the critical fraction. While gasoline gets all the public attention, naphtha is the true engine of the petrochemical industry. It is the primary feedstock for producing the monomers that become plastic. Without naphtha, there is no plastic. And every barrel of refined oil produces it.

◆ What Plastics Actually Are

Plastics are polymers — long chains of repeating molecular units (monomers) derived from petroleum hydrocarbons. The transformation from raw crude oil to the plastic in your water bottle, your car dashboard, your medical device, and your clothing follows a four-step process:

Refining → Cracking → Polymerization → Additives
The four-step transformation from crude oil to consumer plastic
Each step adds value and obscures origin

Step 1: Refining

Crude oil is separated into fractions. Naphtha and natural gas liquids become the starting materials. The petroleum origin is already being obscured — what arrives at the cracking plant is called a "feedstock," not oil.

Step 2: Cracking

Large hydrocarbon molecules are broken down at 750–850°C using steam cracking. This produces smaller, reactive molecules called monomers — ethylene, propylene, styrene, vinyl chloride. The process is energy-intensive, producing significant CO2 emissions, and requires extreme conditions that can only be sustained by massive industrial infrastructure.

Step 3: Polymerization

Monomers are chemically bonded into long polymer chains. Ethylene becomes polyethylene (PE). Propylene becomes polypropylene (PP). Styrene becomes polystyrene (PS). Vinyl chloride becomes polyvinyl chloride (PVC). These are not natural materials. No biological system produces them. No natural enzyme can break them down.

Step 4: Additives

The raw polymer is mixed with additives that determine its properties: plasticizers (phthalates) for flexibility, flame retardants (PBDEs) for fire resistance, colorants, UV stabilizers, and antimicrobials. Many of these additives are toxic. Many are endocrine disruptors. They are not chemically bonded to the polymer — they leach out over time, into your food, your skin, your lungs.

⚠ The Major Plastic Types

  • PE (Polyethylene) — Most produced plastic worldwide. Bags, bottles, films. Found in 75% of human brain microplastics.
  • PP (Polypropylene) — Packaging, automotive parts, textiles. Heat-resistant, used in food containers.
  • PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride) — Pipes, window frames, flooring. Contains chlorine; releases dioxins when burned.
  • PS (Polystyrene) — Styrofoam, disposable cups, packaging. Leaches styrene, a probable carcinogen.
  • PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate) — Water bottles, food containers. Antimony leaching documented at elevated temperatures.
  • Nylon — Textiles, carpets, mechanical parts. First commercially successful synthetic polymer (1935).
  • Polyester — Clothing, textiles, bottles. Now 52% of global fiber production. Sheds microplastics in every wash cycle.

◆ Why Plastics Never Degrade

Plastic is thermodynamically stable. The carbon-carbon backbone of polymer chains is one of the strongest bonds in organic chemistry. Unlike wood, paper, cotton, or food, plastics do not biodegrade — no known natural enzyme can efficiently break down the long-chain hydrocarbons that constitute most commercial plastics.

What does happen is photodegradation — UV radiation from sunlight breaks plastic into smaller and smaller pieces without eliminating it. A plastic bottle does not disappear. It fragments into microplastics (particles smaller than 5mm) and then into nanoplastics (smaller than 1μm). These particles persist indefinitely.

A single plastic bottle takes approximately 450 years to fragment into microplastics. It never becomes nothing. It becomes invisible. — Degradation Timeline Estimates

This is not a design flaw. It is the core engineering property of plastic. The entire value proposition of plastic is that it is durable, resistant to degradation, and permanent. The same property that makes it useful as packaging makes it catastrophic as pollution. And there is no off switch.

◆ The Recycling Myth

The chasing arrows symbol on the bottom of plastic products was invented by the plastics industry in the late 1980s. It was designed to look like the recycling symbol. It was designed to make consumers believe that plastic is recyclable. It is, in most cases, a lie.

As of 2025, Greenpeace reports that only 5–6% of plastic waste in the United States is actually recycled. The rest is landfilled, incinerated, or dumped into the environment. The number has been declining, not improving, as the complexity and contamination of plastic waste increases.

⚠ The Recycling Deception

  • Only 5–6% of US plastic waste is recycled (Greenpeace, 2025).
  • The chasing arrows symbol was created by the Society of the Plastics Industry in 1988 to preempt legislation.
  • The "resin identification codes" (numbers 1–7 inside the triangle) were deliberately designed to resemble recycling symbols.
  • The "Merchants of Myth" report documented industry campaigns to shift responsibility from producers to consumers.
  • Oil companies spent tens of millions on recycling advertising while investing almost nothing in recycling infrastructure.
  • Plastic can typically be recycled only once or twice before quality degrades — it is downcycling, not recycling.
  • Most "recycled" plastic is exported to developing nations where it is burned or dumped.

The recycling myth served its purpose: it prevented regulation, delayed bans, and allowed the plastics industry to continue growing production by 4–6% annually for decades. It shifted blame from the manufacturers to the consumers. You were told to feel guilty for not recycling. The companies producing 400 million tons of plastic per year were never held accountable.

◆ Endocrine Disruptors

Microplastics are only part of the problem. The additives in plastic — the chemicals that make it flexible, fireproof, colorful, and durable — are independently toxic. Many are endocrine disruptors: chemicals that interfere with hormonal systems at extraordinarily low concentrations.

BPA (Bisphenol A) mimics estrogen. It is found in polycarbonate plastics, epoxy resin linings of food cans, and thermal receipt paper. It has been linked to reproductive disorders, obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and cancer. Despite being "banned" in baby products in some countries, it has been largely replaced by BPS and BPF — which show similar or worse endocrine-disrupting properties.

Phthalates are plasticizers that make PVC flexible. They are found in food packaging, medical devices, cosmetics, flooring, and children's toys. They are linked to reduced testosterone, developmental abnormalities, and reproductive harm. They are not chemically bonded to the plastic — they continuously migrate out into whatever they contact.

PFAS ("Forever Chemicals") — per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — are used in non-stick coatings, waterproof fabrics, food packaging, and firefighting foam. They are called "forever chemicals" because no natural process breaks them down. They have been found in the blood of 98% of Americans, in drinking water worldwide, in rainwater on every continent including Antarctica, and in the breast milk of nursing mothers.

The chemical industry has known about the toxicity of these compounds for decades. Internal documents from DuPont, 3M, and others show systematic suppression of health data and deliberate delay of regulation. — Documented in internal industry communications

◆ Petroleum in Everything

Plastic is only one product of petroleum. The full scope of petroleum dependence in modern civilization is almost incomprehensible. Over 365 products are derived from or dependent on petroleum. The modern world does not run on petroleum — it is made of petroleum.

Clothing

Polyester, nylon, acrylic, and spandex are all petroleum-derived synthetic fibers. As of 2024, synthetics account for approximately 69% of global fiber production, with polyester alone at 52%. Every time you wash synthetic clothing, it releases hundreds of thousands of microplastic fibers into the water supply. Your wardrobe is a pollution source.

Cosmetics & Personal Care

Approximately 80% of cosmetics contain petroleum-derived ingredients: mineral oil, paraffin wax, petroleum jelly, synthetic fragrances, and propylene glycol. These are not incidental components — they are the base materials of the products you apply to your skin daily.

Medicine

Pharmaceuticals, medical devices, IV bags, syringes, prosthetics, surgical gloves, and pill coatings are all petroleum products. The modern medical system is structurally dependent on petroleum in ways that make health and petroleum inseparable.

Electronics

Your phone, your computer, your television — all contain petroleum-derived plastics, adhesives, insulators, and circuit board substrates. The device you are reading this on is made of oil.

Building Materials

PVC pipes, insulation foam, vinyl flooring, paint, adhesives, sealants, roofing materials, and carpet fibers. Modern construction is petroleum construction.

Food

The Haber-Bosch process converts natural gas (petroleum) into ammonia, which becomes synthetic fertilizer. Approximately 50% of the world's food production depends on synthetic nitrogen fertilizer. Without petroleum, half the world starves. This is not an accident — it is the intended architecture of dependency.

"Green" Technology

Solar panels, wind turbine blades, electric vehicle components, and battery casings all contain petroleum-derived plastics. The "green transition" is still petroleum-dependent. The industry that claims to be replacing oil is, at the material level, built from it.

You cannot separate petroleum from modern civilization. It is not a resource we use — it is the material substrate of everything we are. That is by design.
